Camera Operation and Filming Techniques
Studio Q
Revised June 2000
After watching one episode, it's pretty obvious that Studio Q (or any other program on COGECO's
community channel) is not a professional production. The difference in picture quality is far from just
the difference between professional and consumer equipment. Sure, SVHS can't stack up against DV
or BetaCam, but the way in which a picture is composed is much more important. Professional TV
shows are moving the cameras around most of the time, creating a much more active, dynamic look.
As well, most of these shows use multiple cameras and only show ten seconds from one camera at a
time. Finally, the production crew will spend a large amount of time setting up the cameras to ensure
proper lighting, framing, and composition.
This guide covers the basic operation of the Panasonic AG-456 video camera, as well as some useful tips and
suggestions for making your work look more professional.
The Panasonic AG-456 SVHS Camera
1. Video and Audio Formats
The AG-456 is an SVHS camera, which achieves a better video
signal from increase resolution, and separating the three
colour channels and the luminance channel into four separate
signals. This boils down to the basic fact that putting an SVHS
tape in a consumer (VHS) machine will not work (the tapes are
the same size, so it is possible). The AG-456 simultaneously
records two separate audio formats ­ normal and hi-fidelity.
Most consumer machines only read the normal signal, while
professional gear will always try to use the hi-fi track, since it
is higher quality.
2. Buttons
No matter what video camera you are using, it's important to know the function of all of the buttons.
Not because you'll use them, but because sometimes things get turned on by accident (time and date
stamp) and it's useful to know how to turn these features off. First, the AG-456 has a record mode
and a playback mode. If the sliding panel on the top of the camera is forward (covering the playback
buttons) the camera is in record mode. The buttons underneath the handle of the camera control
tracking and video format (which should always be in SVHS). The side panel has a whole slew of
buttons that control exposure, focus and digital effects. Finally, there is a switch that controls the
recording mode of the built-in microphone.
3. Roll Up Time
The AG-456 (and most video cameras) makes very clean cuts when recording, due to the fact that
the cameras have a roll-up feature. Basically, when you hit the record button, the camera starts
rolling the tape and waits for it to achieve the proper speed before recording starts ("Index" will flash
in the viewfinder). There is actually a delay of about five seconds between when you hit the record
button and when the camera actually starts recording. Similarly, when you hit the button to stop
recording, the camera stops recording a few seconds later and then winds the tape back. To avoid
any problems of shots being truncated, always give the camera extra time before and after any
action.
4. Focusing
Focusing is done via the ring between the camera hood and the zoom ring. The AG-456 has both
manual and automatic focusing. The button to switch between these two modes is located just next
to the iris control buttons. The iris/shutter control must be set to "manual" or "portrait" in order to
work in manual focus.
i. Auto vs. Manual
Always use manual focus. Although the idea of automatic focus is nice, it's not
one hundred percent reliable. AutoFocus targets whatever is in the center of
the screen ­ not always what you want the camera to focus on. Besides, if
something passes between the camera and what you are focused on, AutoFocus
will attempt to focus on the other object, creating a focus blur. Finally, if you're

ii. Viewfinder
The viewfinder has a separate focus that is in no way connected to the focus of
the lens. You can adjust the focus on the viewfinder by sliding a little knob on
the under side of the viewfinder. Focus the viewfinder so that the text and
icons are in focus, then you'll be able to tell when the main lens is in focus.
5. White
Balance
Colour, as observed by a camera, depends on the light source that illuminates it. This is due in part to the camera
design, but mostly due to physics. Ignoring math and theory, it comes down to this: various light sources operate at
different temperatures, and this changes the colour slightly. The major light sources are sunlight, tungsten (regular
light bulbs) and fluorescent. The AG-456 adjusts for the colour differences through a feature known as "white
balancing" ­ you define white for the camera and it corrects every other colour. To "white balance" the camera, you
need to fill the frame with a white object and hold down the "W.B." button until the icon in the viewfinder stops
flashing (again, like focus, the camera must be in "manual" mode). If you don't have a white wall, or a white sheet
of paper, just put the lens cap on and point the camera at the light source. If the camera balances tungsten as
white, then the sunlight will look blue. Inversely, if the camera decides that sunlight is white, then the tungsten
light will look orange. Fluorescents look green if they aren't balanced properly. To ensure the camera balances
correctly, it's best to use only one type of lighting. If you can do that, you can try and balance the lighting sources
directly, such as using a blue tungsten light bulb to match sunlight.
6. Zoom
Practically every camera in use today comes equipped with a zoom lens, which can simulate all sorts
of standard fixed lenses, such as wide-angle or telephoto. Although a zoom is a nifty effect to play
around with on the camera, you won't see it very often in professional work. It's a very unnatural
effect. Why does the camera have a zoom lens? It's cheaper and more compact than carrying
around a pile of different lenses. It's best to treat the zoom lens as a collection of various fixed
lenses. Zoom the camera to get your subject to the right size and then press the record button. In
the wide-angle setting ("zoomed out") the focus isn't very sensitive. Instead, go to the telephoto
setting ("zoomed in"), focus on your subject, and zoom out to the appropriate size. This will ensure
that the focus is set on your subject, giving it a very crisp image.
7. Headphones
It's very easy to get caught up in making sure the camera is recording video properly, the image
looks good, and so on. However, audio is just as important. The only way to make sure the audio
sounds good, is to use a pair of headphones to listen to what the camera is receiving. The AG-456
has a "Y" shaped cable that fits into the two audio jacks on the back of the camera and allows a pair
of headphones to be plugged in. Check the audio monitor switch on the back of the camera to see
which audio format you are monitoring (Normal, Hi-fi or both ­ "Mix").
8. Tripod
Tripods should be used whenever possible, as they provide the much needed stability for good shots.
Extend the tripod legs all the way out and try to spread the legs as far apart as possible ­ this is the
easiest way to get the camera to the height you want. Most of the tripods have a quick release plate
which screws into the bottom of the camera. The head of the tripod should have knobs to adjust the
resistance to motion in the pan, tilt and pedestal axes. Don't over tighten these knobs when locking
the tripod in place. The same goes for the leg lock knobs. Finally, if you're going to move the
camera (i.e. a pan or tilt) use the lever attached to the tripod head ­ don't push or manipulate the
camera.
If you can't film with a tripod, there are several techniques that will help stabilize your shot. First,
keep the camera in a wide-angle setting ("zoomed out"). Also, try to find something to lean or prop
the camera up against. Try to relax, and keep you elbows and knees bent.
9. Equipment
Care
Whenever you are out on a shoot, make sure you treat the equipment as nicely as possible. New
equipment will remain new longer and old equipment will work longer. To this end, keep the following
tips in mind whenever you are shooting:
i.
Don't Force Anything
If you're getting a lot of resistance from the camera, tripod or other equipment,
chances are you're doing something wrong. For instance, the batteries have a
special notch in them to ensure that the batteries are inserted properly. Trying
to force the battery while in an incorrect alignment will only damage the battery
and the camera.

ii.
Keep Equipment in its Case
If it is in a case in the equipment room, keep it in the case in the field when not
in use. This keeps the equipment clean and dry and protects it from any
impacts.
iii.
Don't Drop Anything
I know it's blatantly obvious point to make, but please don't drop the
equipment! To my knowledge, if it exists and Studio Q owns it, then it's been
dropped, everything from cameras to microphones to lights. Most of those
accidents could have been avoided, such as picking the microphone up if it is
attached to the camera before walking off to shoot something, or simply putting
things in a secure place before leaving them alone.
iv. Cords
One of the biggest problems with the quality of the show is sound. A lot of the
time, it isn't the microphone that is fouling up but the microphone cord and the
connections to the camera and to the microphone. A poorly wrapped cord can
twist and fray inside its casing, creating static noise whenever the cord moves,
and staying still during a shoot is impossible. So always wrap your cords
properly ­ this will keep them in top condition as long as possible and will make
your footage sound much better. A second problem with cords are the
connections, which are wrecked when the camera operator or the reporter
move further than the cords allows, ripping it out of the camera. This damages
both the cord and the camera, which is, of course, obscenely expensive to
repair and virtually inoperative for sound when the connector is damaged.
v.
Clean and Dry
Whenever the weather is nasty (i.e. rain, snow, typical Kingston weather) make
sure that the equipment stays dry and clean. Using the cases is the biggest
contribution to this end, but keeping cameras and mike dry during a rainy shoot
is equally important. If there are camera covers around, then USE THEM! As
well, never put the camera or any piece of equipment down on the ground
unprotected. Dirt in the camera is, well, dirty.
Composition
Composition is simply how you arrange each scene that you shoot with a video camera. While the following rules are not
carved in stone, they help you produce a more professional looking picture. These rules can be broken, but it is important to
know what rule you are breaking and why. This is the difference between amateur and professional work. As a guiding rule,
the idea of composition is to clearly state to the viewer what you are trying to say through the image. Powerful images will
grab a viewer's attention, just like a dynamic speaker will keep an audience's attention. Basically, you're trying to answer the
question:
"What message do I want my picture to deliver?"

2. Movement
Camera movement can be divided into two major categories: stationary and displacement.
Stationary movement refers to zoom, pan and tilt, where the actual location of the camera never
changes. Displacement requires physically moving the camera and refers to dolly, truck and pedestal
motions. During any kind of motion, it is important to have starting and finishing points in mind
Page 4
Page 4 of 7
ahead of time. Most stationary movement is not used in professional shoots, because the created
motion is very unnatural. Finally, if any objects are moving inside the moving frame of the camera,
it's best to let the person/object enter and exit a static frame.
i. zoom, pan, tilt
A zoom creates the illusion of moving forwards by changing the focal length of
the lens. Pans rotate the camera to the left or right, while tilts rotate the
camera up and down. Pans are extremely useful to include an object that won't
fit within a single frame. Make sure you don't spray paint (pan/tilt back and
forth over an object). The speed of a pan or tilt is extremely important, as a
quick motion may come out as a blur.
ii. dolly, truck, pedestal
Moving the camera towards or away from the subject is referred to as a dolly,
while moving the camera from side to side (parallel with the subject) is a truck.
Finally, a pedestal is moving the camera up or down (parallel to the ground). A
truck is also referred to as a track and a pedestal can also be called a crane or
boom.
3. Headroom
Headroom refers to the amount of space between the top of an object (usually a person's head) and
the top of the frame. In general, position the person's eyes one third from the top of the frame. Too
much or too little headroom destroys any proper balance of the composition.
4. Noseroom
Noseroom is basically headroom on the horizontal axis. If a person (or an object with a
distinguishable front and back, such as a car) is looking towards one side of the frame, there should
be more room in front of the person then behind them. Again, as a general rule, the person should
be placed one third of the way in from the opposite side of the frame to which he/she is looking. This
is especially important with any kind of movement. A moving object should always have plenty of
lead room in front of it. This gives the shot a much more relaxing, natural look.
5. Rule of Thirds
Putting noseroom and headroom together gives the rule of thirds. Basically,
divide the viewfinder up using an imaginary tic-tac-toe board to produce nine
squares. Major objects should be placed along these lines. People and
buildings should be placed on the two vertical lines, while horizons should be
placed on the upper or lower horizontal line. In a close-up of a person, the
eyes should be placed on one of the four intersections of the lines.
6. Closure
The edges of the frame create imaginary boundaries, which can easily give the impression that the
object does not continue past the viewfinder of the camera. If these objects do not end, it is
important to visually imply this. The easiest way is to avoid natural cut-off points. For instance, if
you film someone from the chin up, it looks as if his or her head has been decapitated, as the chin is
a natural change in body shape, making the head appear as a self-contained object resting on the
lower edge of the screen. Including part of the neck or chest would provide good closure. Similarly,
never end a frame on a person's elbows, knees or ankles.
7. Contrast and Colour
Brighter, more vibrant colours will always attract the viewer's attention more than dark dull
images. If your foreground object is a bright colourful object, then you're all set. However, if
it is a darker, less vibrant image, try to avoid distracting colours in your background. At the
same time, try to add more light to the foreground (if possible) to brighten the subject and
attract the viewer's attention.
i. Iris
The iris and shutter control the exposure of the picture. The iris determines how much
light enters the lens, and the shutter controls how long the lens is left open. In most
circumstances, adjusting only the iris will be adequate for acquiring proper exposure. An
under exposed shot will look very dark and have noise (green, blue and red dots scattered
throughout the picture). On the other hand, an over exposed shot will look gray and
washed out. With the AG-456 open the iris until the brightest object begins to flare, and
then bring the iris down one setting.
ii. Depth of Field

Page 5 of 7
Simply put, the depth of field is the distance an object can move towards or away from the
camera and still remain in focus. A wide-angle lens has a very large depth of field, while a
telephoto setting has a very small one. A large iris will have a small depth of field, while a
small iris will have a large depth of field. In general having a small depth of field is
extremely useful as it blurs the background, simplifying the image, and attracting the views
attention to the foreground.
iii. Shutter
Speed
In order to achieve a smaller depth of field, you can open the iris by increasing the shutter
speed on the camera. Normally this won't cause a problem unless you are filming a very
rapid action, such as a roller coaster or sporting event (swinging a baseball bat). In these
situations, the higher shutter speed creates an extremely crisp image, which results in
jagged looking motion. A better alternative is to use a neutral density filter, which darkens
the image and allows you to open the iris without over exposing the image.
8. Balance
The concept of balance comes from the idea that you should fill the entire screen with
your object(s) of interest. Generally, unused space is wasted space. If the object
doesn't fill the entire screen, try to balance the image by placing a smaller object in the
unused space.
9. Camera
Height
In most circumstances, the camera is almost always at eye level. In part, this is due to the fact that
as viewer, we are used to seeing the world from this perspective. If the camera is above the eye-line
looking down at a person, it creates a feeling of inferiority or isolation, while looking up at somebody
gives a feeling of superiority and strength. In order to maintain a neutral feeling, you need to
position your camera at the same height as your subject, whether it is a tall basketball player, or
something low to the ground like a dog.
10. Crossing the Line
This can occur whenever there is any back and forth action, such as dialog in a conversation, or a
sporting event such as hockey or tennis. The line of action is an imaginary line, which passes through
both subjects involved in the action, and extends to infinity in both directions. If the camera crosses
this line, it will make it appear that someone is talking to or playing against his or her twin. For
instance, if you are filming someone running and cross his or her line of action (the direction in which
he or she is running) the next shot will look like he or she is running in the opposite direction. If
there are more than two people involved, create multiple lines between each pair and don't cross
them.
Lighting
1. Three Point Lighting
This is basically the industry standard whenever you are interviewing or doing any kind of studio set-
up. Basically, have one bright light in front of the subject, off to one side and usually high up. This is
called the "key light" and lights the majority of the subject. Also in front of the subject, but on the
other side of the camera, is the "fill light". This is a much softer light that reduces the harshness of
any shadows that are cast by the "key light", such as the shadow created by the subject's nose.
Finally, a "back light" is placed behind the subject (usually down low) and helps separate the subject
from the background.
I'm neither an advocate nor opponent of this type of lighting so it's really up to you.
2. Angle of the Light
This certainly plays an important role when shooting outside. The angle the light is cast on a subject
has a huge influence on the mood of the image. Oblique (almost right angles to the line between the
camera and the subject) create a very mysterious look. On a face, lighting from below the eye-line
gives a horror/unnatural feeling. Finally, lighting the subject from behind can create a halo effect, but
make sure the light source isn't directly in-line with the camera. In other words, don't film people in
front of a window ­ you'll get a silhouette.

Sound
Often overlooked, audio is just as important in film and video as the picture itself. About the only trick with audio is having the
right microphone for the situation and using it properly. There are many different types of microphones and each design has
advantages and disadvantages. Just as the zoom lens prevents carrying around multiple fixed lenses, the AG-456's external
microphone can operated in three modes, saving you the hassle of lugging around extra equipment. However, the built-in
microphone can't compete with the quality of a good external specific purpose microphone.
1. Directional vs. Wide
In general, there are only two types of microphones ­ ones that pick up sound that is only in front (directional) and
the other records sound from all over (wide). Wide microphones are used to set the scene by giving the viewer a
broad sense of the sounds in the area, while directional mics are used for the close-up shots (conversation) where
you want to the majority of the recorded sound to come from the object(s) within the frame.
In general, you'll want to get the microphone as close to the subject as possible, without getting the microphone in
the picture. The external microphone on the AG-456 is aimed parallel to the ground at eye-level, so it will normally
capture your subject's audio with no trouble (as well as anything that your subject is standing in front of, whether it
be a crowd, street or building). In order to isolate just your subject's audio, the external microphone is normally
placed low to the ground and pointed to the sky, or conversely, high in the air pointed to the ground. This will
dramatically cut down on any possible background noise.
2. Types of Microphones
i. Hand
Held
The standard field reporter's microphone is good for conversations in quiet locations, but
getting the microphone close enough normally involves having the microphone in the
frame. The cords can get knocked around creating a background crackle or hiss. The
microphone is up and running in a matter of seconds which is a great feature for
spontaneous interviews. These microphones are normally omni directional (wide).
ii. Lavaliere
The "tie-clip" microphone is ideal for interviews, but normally requires extra set-up time to
get it in the right location and hide the cord. Some microphones have a radio transmitter
and receiver, which eliminates the cord problem and allows the subject to move around
without any great difficulty. Again, this microphone is omni directional, so a quiet setting is
important.
iii. Boom The boom microphone (or shotgun) is the most common directional microphone and is
normally mounted on a long pole, so that the microphone can either be placed high in the
air, or low to the ground. This microphone is good for reducing background noise, but it
requires a lot of set-up time, and constant monitoring so that it is always pointed in the
right direction.
Camera Techniques
1. Sequencing
Film or video is basically storytelling, and every story should have a purpose. It starts with a
beginning, develops a thought, and finished with an ending. Film and video should follow the same
pattern. Wide shots (or establishing shots) set the scene and introduce the story to the viewers.
Medium shots focus the views attention to a particular object (or group of objects) without
disorienting the viewer. Finally, close-up shots tell the details of the story.
2. Pull
Focus
Pulling focus is when two objects alternate being in focus at one time. This technique is usually used
in to shift focus between two people in conversation. The camera should have a small depth of field,
and the two objects should be different distances away. The trick is to figure out the two focus
settings ahead of time, so that you can quickly switch focus settings, allowing one object to go out of
focus, while pulling the other one in.
3. Tracking
Tracking is very similar to pulling focus, as it requires a small depth of field and changing the focus
setting. However, tracking keeps one object in focus the entire time while the object moves towards
Page 7
Page 7 of 7
or away from the camera. The secret is to have a few focus settings set ahead of time that are lined
up with landmarks as the object moves towards or away from the camera. With practice its possible
(and faster) to adjust the focus on the fly.
4. Vertigo
Effect
The vertigo effect (used in Alfred Hitchcock's movie "Vertigo", which is where the term came from) is
an attempt to change the spatial relations of the background without altering the foreground, such as
making a road stretch out into the distance. This is accomplished by reducing the depth of field, while
keeping the foreground object the same size. The depth of field is change by zooming the lens to the
telephoto position, and the camera is moved back to keep the foreground object the same size. The
trick is to move the camera back at the same speed that the lens is zooming-in.
5. Scene
Transitions
Scene transitions are equivalent to the punctuation of a story. The camera can tilt down from the sky
to open a scene, and similarly tilt to the sky to end. It's also easy to have the actors and actresses
walk (or jump, fly...) on and off the screen to begin and end the scene. Fading to a solid colour
(usually black) indicates an end, just like a period in a sentence.
6. Whip
Pan
Whip pan is a transition that occurs by blurring the motion, often used in the old Batman TV show.
The camera is quickly panned to one direction at the end of one scene, and this motion is continued
at the start of the next scene. With sound effect or two, this is an effective way to create a distinctive
change of scene.
